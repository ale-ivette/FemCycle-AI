=== MENSTRUAL PHASE (Days 1-5) ===
During menstruation, estrogen and progesterone are at their lowest.
Energy levels are typically reduced. Inflammation may be higher.
RECOMMENDED TRAINING: Low intensity. Yoga, walking, light swimming, mobility work.
AVOID: High intensity intervals, heavy lifting, competitions.
NUTRITION: Iron-rich foods (spinach, lentils, red meat). Anti-inflammatory foods.
SLEEP: Prioritize 8-9 hours. Recovery is critical.
OPEN WATER SWIMMING: Rest or very gentle pool swimming only.
MARATHON TRAINING: Rest or very easy runs. Listen to your body.

=== FOLLICULAR PHASE (Days 6-13) ===
Rising estrogen increases energy, strength, and mood.
This is the best time for high-intensity training and skill acquisition.
RECOMMENDED TRAINING: Strength training, speed work, technical skills, new challenges.
ENDURANCE: Long runs, high mileage weeks, tempo runs.
SWIMMING: Technique focus, sprint sets, open water training.
NUTRITION: Complex carbohydrates for energy. Protein for muscle building.
SLEEP: 7-8 hours sufficient. Recovery is faster in this phase.
MARATHON TRAINING: Long runs, tempo work, speed sessions. Best phase for hard workouts.

=== OVULATION PHASE (Days 14-16) ===
Peak estrogen. Highest energy, strength, and pain tolerance.
Best time for personal records and competitions.
RECOMMENDED TRAINING: Maximum intensity. Race simulations. PRs.
CAUTION: Ligament laxity increases. Warm up thoroughly to avoid injury.
NUTRITION: Maintain carbohydrate intake. Hydration is critical.
SLEEP: 7-8 hours. Energy is high but dont sacrifice sleep.
OPEN WATER SWIMMING: Best phase for distance and speed in open water.
MARATHON TRAINING: Race day ideal. Peak performance window.

=== LUTEAL PHASE (Days 17-28) ===
Progesterone rises. Body temperature increases 0.3-0.5 degrees.
Energy and mood may decrease. PMS symptoms possible in late luteal.
RECOMMENDED TRAINING: Moderate cardio. Reduce volume 20-30%. Focus on technique.
AVOID: Introducing new hard workouts. Back-to-back intense sessions.
NUTRITION: Magnesium-rich foods reduce PMS. Reduce caffeine.
Increase calories slightly (metabolic rate rises 100-300 kcal/day).
SLEEP: May be disrupted. Prioritize sleep hygiene. Magnesium before bed helps.
MARATHON TRAINING: Easy runs only in late luteal. Maintain base, dont push.

=== DIABETES CONSIDERATIONS ===
Blood sugar fluctuates with hormonal changes throughout the cycle.
Luteal phase: insulin resistance increases. Monitor glucose more frequently.
Follicular phase: insulin sensitivity improves. Adjust medication if needed.
Exercise helps regulate blood sugar but intensity must match glucose levels.
Always consult endocrinologist before changing training intensity.
Carry fast-acting glucose during workouts in all phases.

=== ANEMIA CONSIDERATIONS ===
Iron levels drop during menstruation. Risk of anemia highest in menstrual phase.
SYMPTOMS TO WATCH: Unusual fatigue, shortness of breath during easy efforts, pale skin.
NUTRITION: Increase iron-rich foods. Vitamin C improves iron absorption.
TRAINING: Reduce intensity if feeling unusually fatigued. Rest is better than pushing through.
SUPPLEMENT: Consult doctor before iron supplementation.

=== MARATHON TRAINING BY PHASE - DETAILED ===
Follicular + Ovulation (Days 6-16): Long runs, tempo work, speed sessions, hill repeats.
Early Luteal (Days 17-21): Medium long runs, maintain base, moderate intensity.
Late Luteal (Days 22-28): Easy runs only, focus on recovery, reduce volume 30%.
Menstrual (Days 1-5): Rest or very easy runs. Listen to your body above all.
RACE DAY: Schedule races during Follicular or Ovulation phase when possible.

=== OPEN WATER SWIMMING BY PHASE ===
Follicular + Ovulation: Distance sets, speed work, open water sessions, technique drills.
Early Luteal: Moderate distance, technique focus, pool preferred over open water.
Late Luteal: Short sessions, easy pace, prioritize recovery.
Menstrual: Rest or gentle pool swimming. Avoid cold open water in this phase.
SAFETY NOTE: Never swim open water alone regardless of cycle phase.

=== SLEEP AND RECOVERY BY PHASE ===
Follicular: 7-8 hours. Body recovers faster. Can handle back-to-back hard sessions.
Ovulation: 7-8 hours. Peak recovery capacity. Use this window for hard training blocks.
Luteal: 8-9 hours. Progesterone disrupts sleep. Magnesium and consistent bedtime help.
Menstrual: 8-9 hours. Maximum recovery needed. Prioritize sleep over extra training.

=== STRESS AND CORTISOL ===
High stress raises cortisol which disrupts hormonal balance and worsens PMS symptoms.
Luteal phase is most sensitive to stress. Manage workload and emotional load in this phase.
Mindfulness, breathing exercises, and adequate sleep are the most effective interventions.
Overtraining syndrome is more likely when training hard in luteal phase repeatedly.
